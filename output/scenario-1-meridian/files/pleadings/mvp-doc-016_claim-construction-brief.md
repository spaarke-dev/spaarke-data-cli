## I. INTRODUCTION

Pursuant to this Court's Scheduling Order (Dkt. No. 42) and the Patent Local Rules, Plaintiff Meridian Corporation ("Meridian") hereby submits its Opening Claim Construction Brief regarding the disputed claim terms of United States Patent No. 9,876,543 ("the '543 Patent"). The parties have identified two terms requiring construction:

1. **"thermal compression boundary layer"** (Claims 1, 2, 3, 5, 7, 9, 11, and 13)
2. **"multi-stage compression molding"** (Claims 1, 7, and 14)

As demonstrated below, the intrinsic evidence — including the claim language, specification, and prosecution history — provides clear guidance on the meaning of these terms. Meridian's proposed constructions are faithful to the intrinsic record, consistent with how a person of ordinary skill in the art ("POSITA") would understand the terms, and confirmed by relevant extrinsic evidence from technical literature and expert testimony.

---

## II. LEGAL STANDARD FOR CLAIM CONSTRUCTION

Claim construction is a matter of law for the Court. *Markman v. Westview Instruments, Inc.*, 517 U.S. 370, 391 (1996). Claims are construed from the perspective of a person of ordinary skill in the art at the time of the invention. *Phillips v. AWH Corp.*, 415 F.3d 1303, 1313 (Fed. Cir. 2005) (en banc).

The Court applies the *Phillips* framework, examining the intrinsic evidence in the following order of priority:

1. **Claim language:** The words of the claim define the scope of the patent right. *Id.* at 1312.
2. **Specification:** The specification is "the single best guide to the meaning of a disputed term." *Id.* at 1315 (quoting *Vitronics Corp. v. Conceptronic, Inc.*, 90 F.3d 1576, 1582 (Fed. Cir. 1996)).
3. **Prosecution history:** The prosecution history provides evidence of how the inventor and the PTO understood the patent. *Id.* at 1317.

Extrinsic evidence — including expert testimony, dictionaries, and learned treatises — may be considered to assist the Court in understanding the technology, but cannot override the intrinsic record. *Id.* at 1318-19.

A person of ordinary skill in the art of the '543 Patent would have: (a) a Ph.D. or equivalent in materials science, mechanical engineering, or a related field; (b) at least five years of experience in composite material processing or thermal compression molding technology; and (c) familiarity with manufacturing process control systems.

## III. DISPUTED TERM 1: "THERMAL COMPRESSION BOUNDARY LAYER"

### A. The Parties' Proposed Constructions

| Party | Proposed Construction |
|-------|----------------------|
| **Meridian** | "The thermally managed zone at the interface between the compression tooling and the composite workpiece, where controlled heat transfer and pressure distribution occur during compression." |
| **Pinnacle** | "A distinct physical layer of material formed at the surface of the workpiece through the application of heat and pressure, having measurably different properties from the bulk material." |

### B. Meridian's Construction Is Correct

#### 1. The Claim Language

The term "thermal compression boundary layer" appears in Claims 1, 2, 3, 5, 7, 9, 11, and 13 of the '543 Patent. Claim 1, the broadest independent claim, recites:

> "a thermal management subsystem configured to establish and maintain a thermal compression boundary layer at the interface between the compression assembly and the workpiece during each compression stage"

The claim language itself indicates that the "thermal compression boundary layer" is (a) established and maintained by the thermal management subsystem; (b) located "at the interface between the compression assembly and the workpiece"; and (c) present "during each compression stage." The term describes a *zone* or *region* at the tooling-workpiece interface, not a permanent material layer in the workpiece.

#### 2. The Specification

The specification provides extensive support for Meridian's construction. The '543 Patent specification describes the "thermal compression boundary layer" as follows:

> "The thermal compression boundary layer is the controlled thermal zone established at the tooling-workpiece interface during compression. Within this zone, heat transfer from the tooling to the workpiece and pressure distribution across the workpiece surface are precisely managed to achieve optimal material flow and consolidation." ('543 Patent, Col. 4, ll. 32-39.)

The specification further explains:

> "Unlike conventional molding processes that rely on bulk heating of the entire workpiece, the present invention establishes a precisely controlled boundary layer where temperature gradients and pressure profiles are independently managed. This boundary layer approach enables superior control over resin flow, fiber alignment, and void elimination." ('543 Patent, Col. 5, ll. 14-21.)

The specification also describes the boundary layer in dynamic terms:

> "The thermal compression boundary layer is not a static phenomenon but rather a dynamic zone whose characteristics — including thickness, temperature gradient, and pressure profile — are continuously adjusted by the control system during each compression stage and during transitions between stages." ('543 Patent, Col. 6, ll. 45-51.)

These passages make clear that the "thermal compression boundary layer" is a dynamically controlled zone at the tooling-workpiece interface, not a permanent material layer in the workpiece itself.

#### 3. The Prosecution History

During prosecution, the applicant distinguished the claimed "thermal compression boundary layer" from prior art thermal molding processes. In the Response to Office Action dated September 14, 2021, the applicant stated:

> "The claimed thermal compression boundary layer is fundamentally different from the bulk heating approach of [the cited prior art]. The boundary layer of the present invention is a controlled zone at the tooling-workpiece interface where heat transfer and pressure are independently managed, enabling multi-stage processing with optimized transitions between stages."

This statement confirms that the boundary layer is a process zone, not a material layer.

#### 4. Pinnacle's Construction Is Incorrect

Pinnacle's proposed construction — "a distinct physical layer of material formed at the surface of the workpiece through the application of heat and pressure, having measurably different properties from the bulk material" — improperly narrows the claim by requiring a permanent physical layer with "measurably different properties." Nothing in the intrinsic record supports this limitation.

The specification never describes the thermal compression boundary layer as a permanent material layer with different properties. To the contrary, the specification describes it as a dynamic, controlled zone that exists during the compression process. Pinnacle's construction would read out the dynamic, process-control aspects of the invention that are central to the patent.

Moreover, Pinnacle's construction conflicts with dependent Claim 2, which recites "wherein the thermal compression boundary layer has a thickness that varies dynamically during compression." A permanent material layer does not have a "dynamically varying thickness" — a process zone does.

## IV. DISPUTED TERM 2: "MULTI-STAGE COMPRESSION MOLDING"

### A. The Parties' Proposed Constructions

| Party | Proposed Construction |
|-------|----------------------|
| **Meridian** | "A compression process comprising two or more discrete compression stages applied sequentially to the workpiece, each stage having independently controllable parameters." |
| **Pinnacle** | "A compression process requiring at least three distinct compression stages, each separated by a complete pressure release and re-application cycle, with each stage having fundamentally different temperature and pressure parameters." |

### B. Meridian's Construction Is Correct

#### 1. The Claim Language

Claim 1 recites "a multi-stage compression assembly configured to apply sequential compression forces." The prefix "multi-" means "more than one" or "many." *See* Webster's Third New International Dictionary (defining "multi-" as "many: multiple"). The claim language requires only "two or more" stages, not a specific minimum number.

Claim 7, the independent method claim, recites "establishing a thermal compression boundary layer at a first temperature and a first compression force" and "transitioning to a second compression stage." This language confirms that "multi-stage" encompasses as few as two stages.

#### 2. The Specification

The specification describes embodiments with varying numbers of stages:

> "In one embodiment, the system performs a two-stage compression process, with a first stage for initial material consolidation and a second stage for final compaction and surface finishing." ('543 Patent, Col. 7, ll. 22-26.)

> "In another embodiment, the system performs a three-stage or four-stage compression process, with intermediate stages for controlled resin flow and fiber realignment." ('543 Patent, Col. 7, ll. 27-30.)

> "The number of compression stages may be selected based on the complexity of the workpiece geometry and the properties of the composite material being processed." ('543 Patent, Col. 7, ll. 31-34.)

These passages confirm that the patent contemplates two or more stages, with the number determined by the application.

The specification also describes each stage as having "independently controllable parameters":

> "Each compression stage is characterized by independently settable parameters including, but not limited to, compression force, compression rate, dwell time, temperature setpoint, and temperature ramp rate." ('543 Patent, Col. 8, ll. 5-9.)

#### 3. The Prosecution History

The prosecution history does not narrow the meaning of "multi-stage compression molding" beyond its plain meaning. The applicant did not make any representations limiting the term to three or more stages, or requiring complete pressure release between stages.

#### 4. Pinnacle's Construction Is Incorrect

Pinnacle's proposed construction improperly imports three limitations not found in the claims or specification:

**(a) "At least three distinct compression stages."** The specification expressly describes a two-stage embodiment. Pinnacle's construction would exclude an embodiment disclosed in the specification, which is disfavored. *Oatey Co. v. IPS Corp.*, 514 F.3d 1271, 1277 (Fed. Cir. 2008).

**(b) "Complete pressure release and re-application cycle."** Nothing in the intrinsic record requires a complete pressure release between stages. The specification describes "controlled transitions between stages" (Col. 8, l. 22), which encompasses gradual pressure changes without full release.

**(c) "Fundamentally different temperature and pressure parameters."** The claims require only "independently controllable parameters," not "fundamentally different" parameters. Two stages could have similar but not identical parameters and still constitute distinct stages.

Pinnacle's construction is an attempt to narrow the claims to avoid infringement by the AutoForge System, which Pinnacle contends uses only a "two-stage" process. This is impermissible claim narrowing.

## V. EXTRINSIC EVIDENCE

### A. Expert Testimony

Dr. Emily Nakamura, Meridian's claim construction expert, has submitted a declaration confirming that Meridian's proposed constructions are consistent with how a POSITA would understand the disputed terms. (Nakamura Decl. Paragraphs 12-45.)

Dr. Nakamura opines that the term "thermal compression boundary layer" is well understood in the materials science community as referring to a process zone, not a permanent material layer:

> "In the field of thermal compression processing, the term 'boundary layer' refers to the region at the interface where thermal and mechanical conditions differ from the bulk conditions. It is a dynamic zone that exists during processing, not a permanent feature of the finished workpiece." (Nakamura Decl. Paragraph 18.)

### B. Technical Literature

Multiple technical references support Meridian's construction:

1. Zhang, et al., "Thermal Boundary Layer Analysis in Compression Molding of Thermoplastic Composites," *Journal of Composite Materials*, Vol. 52, No. 8 (2018), describing the "thermal boundary layer" as "the interfacial zone where temperature gradients are steepest during compression processing."

2. Morrison & Chen, "Multi-Stage Compression Processing of Advanced Composites: A Review," *Composites Part A*, Vol. 134 (2020), defining "multi-stage compression" as "any compression molding process employing two or more sequential compression stages with independently controlled process parameters."

3. Williams, "Process Control in Thermal Compression Molding," *SAMPE Conference Proceedings* (2019), describing boundary layer management as "dynamic control of the thermal and mechanical conditions at the tooling-workpiece interface during compression."

---

## VI. CONCLUSION

For the reasons set forth above, Meridian respectfully requests that this Court adopt the following claim constructions:

1. **"Thermal compression boundary layer"**: "The thermally managed zone at the interface between the compression tooling and the composite workpiece, where controlled heat transfer and pressure distribution occur during compression."

2. **"Multi-stage compression molding"**: "A compression process comprising two or more discrete compression stages applied sequentially to the workpiece, each stage having independently controllable parameters."
