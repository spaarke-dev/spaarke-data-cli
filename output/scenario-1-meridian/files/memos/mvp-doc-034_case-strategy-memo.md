## I. PURPOSE

This memorandum sets forth our recommended litigation strategy for the patent infringement action against Pinnacle Industries. It analyzes the strengths and weaknesses of our position, evaluates strategic options, and provides recommendations for prosecuting this case through trial or settlement.

## II. CASE OVERVIEW

We filed this action on August 15, 2025, alleging that Pinnacle's AutoForge Multi-Stage Compression Platform infringes Claims 1-14 of US Patent 9,876,543. The case is assigned to Judge Margaret L. Strauss in the Northern District of California, San Jose Division. Judge Strauss has significant patent litigation experience and is known for efficient case management.

The initial case management conference is scheduled for October 6, 2025. We anticipate a scheduling order will issue shortly thereafter, with a trial date likely in mid-to-late 2026.

## III. STRENGTHS OF OUR POSITION

### A. Strong Infringement Case

Our infringement case is exceptionally strong. Pinnacle had direct access to the patented technology through the Technology License Agreement and received the complete Technical Package. Their AutoForge System was developed after receiving this technology, and preliminary analysis of publicly available materials shows striking similarities to the claimed invention.

The fact that Pinnacle's chief engineer, Dr. Thomas Brennan, was personally involved in receiving the Technical Package training from Meridian and subsequently led the AutoForge development team creates a compelling narrative of misappropriation.

### B. Willfulness

We have strong evidence of willful infringement. Pinnacle had actual knowledge of the '543 Patent through the TLA, which expressly referenced the patent. Despite this knowledge, Pinnacle developed and marketed the AutoForge System outside the licensed Field of Use. The June 2025 cease-and-desist letter further establishes Pinnacle's post-filing knowledge. Willfulness supports enhanced damages up to three times compensatory damages under 35 U.S.C. Section 284.

### C. Contractual Breach as Corroboration

The parallel breach of the Technology License Agreement corroborates our infringement theory. Pinnacle's expansion beyond the automotive heat exchanger field of use into aerospace and defense demonstrates both unauthorized use of the technology and the commercial value of the patented invention.

### D. Favorable Forum and Judge

Judge Strauss has presided over several significant patent cases and has a track record of carefully considered claim construction orders. She is not known to favor either plaintiffs or defendants, which benefits us given the strength of our case.

## IV. WEAKNESSES AND RISKS

### A. Invalidity Defense

Pinnacle will almost certainly assert invalidity defenses. Our preliminary prior art search identified several references that relate to thermal compression molding, though none that anticipate or render obvious the specific multi-stage approach with boundary layer management claimed in the '543 Patent. The most concerning references are:

1. **Yamamoto (2019)** — Describes a two-stage compression process for thermoplastic composites but does not disclose thermal compression boundary layer management.
2. **European Patent EP 2,345,678** — Discloses a thermal management system for compression molding but uses a fundamentally different approach (bulk heating with localized cooling, rather than boundary layer management).

We assess invalidity risk as **medium**. Dr. Emily Nakamura's preliminary validity analysis supports patentability over the identified prior art, but Pinnacle may uncover additional references during its own search.

### B. Claim Construction Uncertainty

Two key terms require construction: "thermal compression boundary layer" and "multi-stage compression molding." While we believe the intrinsic evidence strongly supports our proposed constructions, Pinnacle will argue for narrow constructions designed to exclude the AutoForge System. Claim construction is inherently unpredictable, and an adverse ruling could significantly weaken our infringement case.

### C. Damages Calculation Complexity

Calculating damages in this case involves complexity. We must choose between lost profits and reasonable royalty theories (or a combination). Lost profits require proof that Meridian would have made the sales captured by Pinnacle, which may be difficult given that some AutoForge customers were in markets Meridian does not currently serve (aerospace/defense). A reasonable royalty analysis based on a hypothetical negotiation is more straightforward but may yield a lower damages figure.

### D. Pinnacle's Financial Resources

Pinnacle is a well-funded company with annual revenues exceeding $200 million. They will have resources to mount an aggressive defense, retain top experts, and potentially outlast us in discovery. We should be prepared for a hard-fought case.

## V. STRATEGIC OPTIONS

### Option A: Aggressive Prosecution Through Trial

**Approach:** Pursue the case aggressively with the goal of obtaining a jury verdict, including enhanced damages for willfulness and an injunction.

**Pros:** Maximizes potential recovery; establishes strong deterrent; vindicates Meridian's patent rights; potential for injunction that removes AutoForge from the market.

**Cons:** Highest cost ($3-5M through trial); longest timeline (12-18 months); verdict uncertainty; appellate risk.

**Recommended if:** Settlement negotiations fail or Pinnacle offers inadequate terms.

### Option B: Early Settlement

**Approach:** Initiate settlement discussions before significant discovery costs are incurred.

**Pros:** Fastest resolution; lowest cost; preserves business relationship; certainty of outcome.

**Cons:** May yield lower recovery; does not establish judicial precedent; Pinnacle may perceive weakness.

**Recommended if:** Pinnacle makes a reasonable offer (see settlement analysis in separate memo).

### Option C: Hybrid — Litigate to Markman, Then Settle

**Approach:** Aggressively litigate through claim construction, then use a favorable Markman ruling as leverage for settlement negotiations.

**Pros:** Favorable claim construction significantly increases settlement leverage; moderate cost ($1.5-2.5M through Markman); demonstrates seriousness; preserves trial option.

**Cons:** Adverse Markman ruling weakens settlement position; moderate cost and timeline; claim construction is inherently uncertain.

**Recommended:** **This is our recommended strategy.** It balances cost, risk, and potential recovery.

## VI. RECOMMENDED APPROACH

We recommend **Option C** — the hybrid strategy. Specifically:

1. **Months 1-3 (Sep-Nov 2025):** Propound aggressive written discovery; identify and retain experts; prepare claim construction positions.

2. **Months 4-6 (Dec 2025-Feb 2026):** Complete fact discovery on liability issues; exchange claim construction briefs; prepare for Markman hearing.

3. **Month 7 (Mar 2026):** Markman hearing. If ruling is favorable, immediately initiate settlement discussions with enhanced leverage.

4. **Months 8-10 (Apr-Jun 2026):** If settlement fails, complete expert discovery; file motion for partial summary judgment on infringement; prepare for trial.

5. **Months 11-14 (Jul-Oct 2026):** Trial preparation and trial.
