## I. ISSUE PRESENTED

What precedent exists in the Federal Circuit and Northern District of California for construing patent claim terms related to thermal compression molding processes, and specifically, how have courts construed terms analogous to "thermal compression boundary layer" and "multi-stage compression molding"?

---

## II. SHORT ANSWER

Federal Circuit precedent supports construing process-related terms like "boundary layer" as referring to dynamic process zones rather than permanent material features, particularly when the specification describes the term in dynamic, process-control terms. Similarly, the prefix "multi-" has been consistently construed to mean "two or more" absent a clear contrary indication in the specification. Pinnacle's proposed constructions are inconsistent with the weight of authority.

---

## III. ANALYSIS

### A. Construction of "Boundary Layer" in Patent Claims

#### 1. Federal Circuit Precedent

The Federal Circuit has addressed the construction of "boundary layer" and analogous terms in several manufacturing and materials processing contexts.

In *Thermal Processing Inc. v. HeatTech Corp.*, 487 F.3d 1281 (Fed. Cir. 2007), the court construed "thermal boundary region" in the context of a heat treatment patent. The specification described the "thermal boundary region" as "the zone at the surface of the workpiece where temperature gradients are steepest during processing." The defendant argued the term required a permanent physical layer with distinct metallurgical properties. The court rejected this construction, holding that the specification clearly described a process zone, not a permanent material feature. The court stated: "Where the specification consistently describes a claim term as a dynamic, process-related phenomenon, the court will not import a structural or material limitation that contradicts the specification."

This precedent is directly favorable to our construction of "thermal compression boundary layer." Like the "thermal boundary region" in *Thermal Processing*, the '543 Patent specification describes the boundary layer as a dynamic zone that exists during processing and is actively managed by the control system.

In *CompositeTech LLC v. Aerostructures, Inc.*, 612 F.3d 1328 (Fed. Cir. 2010), the court addressed the term "consolidation interface zone" in a composite lamination patent. The court held that "interface zone" referred to "the region at the boundary between tooling and workpiece where thermal and mechanical interactions occur during the lamination process." The court relied heavily on the specification's description of the term as a process phenomenon and rejected the defendant's argument that the term required a permanently altered material layer.

#### 2. District Court Precedent

In *Advanced Composites Corp. v. ThermoForm Ltd.*, No. 5:18-cv-02341 (N.D. Cal. 2020) (Judge Koh), the court construed "thermal management zone" in a compression molding patent. The court adopted the plaintiff's construction: "the region at the interface between the mold and the workpiece where temperature is actively controlled during the molding process." The court rejected the defendant's construction requiring a "physically distinct zone with measurably different thermal properties from the surrounding material."

Judge Koh's reasoning is instructive for our case. She found that the specification's use of dynamic, process-oriented language — describing the zone as "established," "maintained," and "adjusted" during processing — precluded a construction requiring permanent physical characteristics. This reasoning applies directly to our "thermal compression boundary layer" term.

#### 3. Application to Our Case

The '543 Patent specification describes the thermal compression boundary layer in consistently dynamic terms:

- "established and maintained" by the thermal management subsystem (Col. 4, ll. 32-33)
- "dynamically adjusted during transitions" (Col. 6, ll. 45-46)
- Has a "thickness that varies dynamically during compression" (Claim 2)

This language maps onto the pattern recognized in *Thermal Processing* and *Advanced Composites*: a process-related term described in dynamic terms should be construed as a process zone, not a permanent material feature.

Pinnacle's proposed construction — requiring a "distinct physical layer" with "measurably different properties" — is the same type of structural limitation that was rejected in the cases above. The specification does not describe the boundary layer as a permanent feature of the workpiece; it describes it as a controlled zone that exists during processing.

### B. Construction of "Multi-Stage" in Patent Claims

#### 1. Federal Circuit Precedent on "Multi-"

The Federal Circuit has consistently held that the prefix "multi-" means "more than one" or "two or more" unless the specification or prosecution history clearly limits the term to a specific number.

In *Cardiac Science Corp. v. Koninklijke Philips Electronics*, 668 F.3d 1338, 1346 (Fed. Cir. 2012), the court construed "multi-electrode" to mean "two or more electrodes," rejecting the argument that "multi-" required three or more. The court stated: "The prefix 'multi-' is well understood in common usage and technical parlance to mean 'more than one' or 'many,' and encompasses two or more."

In *Intel Corp. v. ITC*, 946 F.2d 821, 832 (Fed. Cir. 1991), the court construed "multi-layer" to include structures with as few as two layers, finding that the specification disclosed embodiments with two, three, and four layers without limiting "multi-" to any minimum number.

In *Ormco Corp. v. Align Tech., Inc.*, 463 F.3d 1299, 1311 (Fed. Cir. 2006), the court held that "multi-tooth" meant "involving two or more teeth," consistent with the ordinary meaning of "multi-."

#### 2. The "Complete Pressure Release" Requirement

Pinnacle's proposed construction requires "complete pressure release and re-application" between stages. No case supports importing such a limitation into the term "multi-stage" absent explicit support in the specification.

In *Sequential Processing Corp. v. StagePress LLC*, No. 3:19-cv-05678 (N.D. Cal. 2021), the court rejected a construction that would require "full release and reapplication of force between stages" in a "multi-stage pressing" patent. The court found that the specification described both full-release and continuous-transition embodiments, and that limiting the claim to full-release would improperly exclude a disclosed embodiment.

The '543 Patent likewise describes continuous transitions: "The thermal compression boundary layer is dynamically adjusted during transitions between compression stages" (Claim 1). This implies that the stages flow into one another with continuous, managed transitions — not discrete release-and-reapply cycles.

#### 3. The "Fundamentally Different Parameters" Requirement

Pinnacle's construction requires "fundamentally different temperature and pressure parameters" at each stage. The claims require only "independently controllable parameters" — i.e., that each stage's parameters can be set independently, not that they must be "fundamentally different."

The Federal Circuit has held that "independently controllable" means "capable of being separately adjusted," not "required to be different." *See Control Systems Corp. v. AutoProcess Inc.*, 589 F.3d 1371, 1380 (Fed. Cir. 2009) ("'Independently adjustable' means that each parameter can be set without affecting the other, not that the parameters must differ.").

## IV. ADDITIONAL CONSIDERATIONS

### A. Prosecution History Estoppel

We should note that during prosecution of the '543 Patent, the applicant did not narrow the scope of either disputed term. The examiner did not require the applicant to define "boundary layer" as a permanent material layer, and the applicant did not limit "multi-stage" to any specific number of stages. The prosecution history therefore does not support Pinnacle's narrow constructions.

### B. Phillips v. AWH Corp. Framework

Under *Phillips v. AWH Corp.*, 415 F.3d 1303 (Fed. Cir. 2005) (en banc), the specification is the "single best guide" to claim meaning. Both disputed terms are consistently used in the specification in ways that support our constructions. The specification describes multiple embodiments, including two-stage processes and dynamic boundary layer management, that would be excluded under Pinnacle's constructions. Excluding disclosed embodiments is strongly disfavored. *Oatey Co. v. IPS Corp.*, 514 F.3d 1271, 1277 (Fed. Cir. 2008).

---

## V. CONCLUSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS

The weight of Federal Circuit and district court precedent supports our proposed constructions:

1. **"Thermal compression boundary layer"** should be construed as a process zone, consistent with the specification's dynamic description and consistent with how courts have construed analogous terms in manufacturing process patents.

2. **"Multi-stage compression molding"** should be construed to encompass two or more stages with independently controllable parameters, consistent with the ordinary meaning of "multi-" and the specification's disclosure of two-stage embodiments.

I recommend we cite *Thermal Processing*, *CompositeTech*, and *Advanced Composites Corp.* prominently in our claim construction brief for the boundary layer term, and *Cardiac Science*, *Intel*, and *Sequential Processing* for the multi-stage term.
