### B. Analysis of Each Reference

#### Reference D1: Yamamoto (2019)

12. Yamamoto describes a two-stage compression process for carbon fiber reinforced thermoplastic composites. The paper presents a process where an initial compression stage at elevated temperature consolidates the material, followed by a second compression stage at reduced temperature for final forming.

13. **What D1 discloses:** A two-stage compression process with different temperature and pressure parameters at each stage. The paper demonstrates improved mechanical properties compared to single-stage compression.

14. **What D1 does not disclose:**

   a. *Thermal compression boundary layer management.* Yamamoto uses conventional bulk heating of the mold, with no discussion of establishing or maintaining a controlled thermal zone at the tooling-workpiece interface. The entire mold and workpiece are heated to a uniform target temperature in each stage.

   b. *Dynamic adjustment of the boundary layer during stage transitions.* Because Yamamoto does not create a boundary layer, there is no dynamic adjustment of boundary layer characteristics during transitions.

   c. *Independent temperature profile and compression force control during each stage.* While Yamamoto uses different temperature and force values for each stage, the paper does not disclose a control system that independently regulates temperature profiles in real time. The temperatures are pre-set and held constant during each stage.

15. **Conclusion on D1:** Yamamoto does not anticipate any claim of the '543 Patent and is missing at least three elements required by Claim 1.

#### Reference D2: EP 2,345,678 (Mueller, 2017)

16. Mueller discloses a thermal management system for compression molding of fiber-reinforced composites. The Mueller system uses a combination of bulk mold heating and localized cooling channels to manage temperature distribution during compression.

17. **What D2 discloses:** A thermal management system with localized cooling to control temperature distribution. A single-stage compression process with thermal control.

18. **What D2 does not disclose:**

   a. *Multi-stage compression.* Mueller describes a single-stage compression process. There is no disclosure of sequential compression stages with independently controllable parameters.

   b. *Thermal compression boundary layer.* Mueller's system uses bulk heating with localized cooling to achieve uniform temperature distribution — the opposite of boundary layer management. Mueller's goal is to eliminate temperature gradients, not to establish and maintain a controlled thermal zone at the interface.

   c. *Dynamic boundary layer adjustment during stage transitions.* Not applicable, as Mueller has neither a boundary layer nor multiple stages.

19. **Conclusion on D2:** Mueller does not anticipate any claim and is missing the multi-stage and boundary layer elements.

#### Reference D3: Chen & Park (2018)

20. Chen & Park describe a dynamic process control system for thermoset compression molding that uses real-time sensor feedback to adjust compression force during a single-stage molding cycle.

21. **What D3 discloses:** A control system with real-time sensor feedback for adjusting compression parameters. A sensor array for monitoring temperature and pressure during compression.

22. **What D3 does not disclose:** Multi-stage compression. Thermal compression boundary layer management. The system adjusts parameters within a single continuous compression stage, not across discrete stages.

23. **Conclusion on D3:** Does not anticipate. Missing multi-stage and boundary layer elements.

#### References D4-D8: Summary Analysis

24. I have analyzed References D4 through D8 in the same element-by-element manner. The detailed analysis is provided in Exhibit C. In summary:

   - **D4 (Anderson):** Discloses a heated mold with temperature control zones, but uses a single compression stage and does not manage a boundary layer at the tooling-workpiece interface.

   - **D5 (Kowalski):** Discusses thermal interface materials in composite tooling but in the context of autoclave processing, not compression molding. Does not disclose multi-stage compression or boundary layer management during compression.

   - **D6 (Tanaka):** Describes a multi-stage press for metal forming (not composite materials). The thermal management approach is fundamentally different from boundary layer management — Tanaka heats the workpiece before pressing, not at the tooling-workpiece interface during compression.

   - **D7 (Williams):** Discloses a control system for compression molding with programmable compression profiles. However, the system operates in a single continuous compression cycle with varying force levels, not discrete stages. There is no boundary layer management.

   - **D8 (Singh & Gupta):** Describes multi-stage processing of thermoplastic composites, but the "stages" are separate processing operations (extrusion, forming, consolidation) performed on different equipment, not sequential compression stages on a single press with boundary layer management.

25. **None of the eight references individually anticipates any claim of the '543 Patent.** Each reference is missing at least two of the key elements recited in Claim 1.

## VI. OBVIOUSNESS ANALYSIS

26. Pinnacle's Invalidity Contentions assert that Claims 1-14 are rendered obvious by combinations of the cited references. Pinnacle primarily relies on the following combinations:

   - D1 (Yamamoto) + D2 (Mueller)
   - D1 (Yamamoto) + D3 (Chen & Park) + D5 (Kowalski)
   - D6 (Tanaka) + D2 (Mueller) + D3 (Chen & Park)

### A. D1 + D2 Combination

27. Pinnacle argues that a POSITA would combine Yamamoto's multi-stage compression approach with Mueller's thermal management system to arrive at the '543 Patent's claimed invention.

28. This combination fails for two reasons:

29. **First, no motivation to combine.** Yamamoto and Mueller address different problems and operate on different principles. Yamamoto uses bulk heating with staged compression; Mueller uses localized cooling to achieve uniform temperature. A POSITA would not be motivated to combine a system designed to create temperature gradients (staged compression at different temperatures) with a system designed to eliminate temperature gradients (uniform cooling). The approaches are fundamentally incompatible.

30. **Second, the combination does not yield the claimed invention.** Even if combined, Yamamoto's two-stage compression with Mueller's localized cooling does not produce a thermal compression boundary layer that is "dynamically adjusted during transitions between compression stages." Mueller's cooling system is designed for steady-state thermal management, not dynamic adjustment during stage transitions.

### B. D1 + D3 + D5 Combination

31. This three-reference combination likewise fails. There is no motivation to combine a multi-stage compression process (D1), a single-stage dynamic control system (D3), and autoclave thermal interface materials (D5). The references address different manufacturing contexts and would not lead a POSITA to the specific combination of multi-stage compression with boundary layer management as claimed.

### C. D6 + D2 + D3 Combination

32. This combination fails because D6 (Tanaka) relates to metal forming, not composite processing. A POSITA working in composite thermal compression molding would not look to metal forming press technology as a starting point. Furthermore, even combining all three references does not yield the claimed dynamic boundary layer adjustment during stage transitions.

### D. Secondary Considerations of Non-Obviousness

33. Several secondary considerations support the non-obviousness of the '543 Patent:

   a. **Commercial Success:** The thermal compression molding technology of the '543 Patent has been commercially successful, as demonstrated by the multi-million dollar licensing relationship with Pinnacle and Pinnacle's own commercial success with the AutoForge System (which practices the patent).

   b. **Long-Felt Need:** The composite manufacturing industry had long sought improved methods for controlling material flow and consolidation in compression molding. The '543 Patent's boundary layer management approach addressed this need.

   c. **Failure of Others:** Prior attempts to improve compression molding focused on either better bulk heating (Mueller) or mechanical compression control (Chen & Park), but did not achieve the level of process control enabled by the '543 Patent's boundary layer approach.

   d. **Copying:** Pinnacle's development of the AutoForge System using Meridian's Technical Package is strong evidence that the invention was not obvious — if the solution were obvious, Pinnacle would not have needed Meridian's proprietary information to develop it.

## VII. CLAIM CONSTRUCTION OPINIONS

34. I was asked to provide opinions on the proper construction of "thermal compression boundary layer" and "multi-stage compression molding." This Court has already construed these terms (Dkt. No. 89). I confirm that the Court's constructions are consistent with how a POSITA would understand these terms.

35. **"Thermal compression boundary layer"** — The Court's construction ("the thermally managed zone at the interface between the compression tooling and the composite workpiece, where controlled heat transfer and pressure distribution occur during compression") correctly identifies the boundary layer as a process zone, not a permanent material layer. This is consistent with the use of "boundary layer" in the broader engineering literature, where the term refers to a region of transition between two media (e.g., the thermal boundary layer in fluid mechanics).

36. **"Multi-stage compression molding"** — The Court's construction ("a compression process comprising two or more discrete compression stages applied sequentially to the workpiece, each stage having independently controllable parameters") correctly interprets "multi-stage" as requiring two or more stages. The prefix "multi-" in technical literature and common usage means "more than one" or "multiple," encompassing two or more.
